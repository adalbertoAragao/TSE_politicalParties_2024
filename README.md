This study investigates distributive, inferential, and structural properties associated with the financial transactions of Brazilian political parties during the 2024 fiscal year, using publicly available data provided by the Brazilian Superior Electoral Court (TSE). The research addresses the problem of high heterogeneity in real-world financial datasets, characterized by strong asymmetry, value concentration, and heavy-tailed distributions, conditions that challenge conventional statistical and computational procedures. Methodologically, the study integrates distributed processing techniques in a PySpark environment, descriptive and inferential statistics, Monte Carlo simulation, stratified sampling based on empirical quantiles, robust outlier detection, unsupervised clustering through K-Means, and structural graph analysis. The results revealed strong financial concentration, with a small fraction of observations accounting for most of the aggregated monetary value in the dataset. Logarithmic transformation proved essential for variance stabilization and data adequacy throughout subsequent analytical stages. Inferential experiments demonstrated that weighted stratified sampling outperformed Simple Random Sampling under highly heterogeneous distributive conditions. Outlier analysis revealed behavior compatible with Pareto-type distributions, while clustering procedures identified distinct distributive regimes associated with the financial magnitude of transactions. Additionally, graph analysis revealed centralized relational structures within extreme financial flows. Overall, the findings demonstrate the relevance of integrating statistical inference, outlier detection, and structural analysis for investigating complex financial flows in massive data environments.
